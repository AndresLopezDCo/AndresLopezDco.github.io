Ecological Footprint dashboard - Tableau

In this project, I investigated the ecological footprint of various countries to understand their impact on the environment. The ecological footprint serves as a critical metric, quantifying the balance between the resources a population consumes and the Earth's capacity to regenerate those resources. Understanding this balance is essential for addressing environmental sustainability and resource management challenges.

Data Source

The data was sourced from an Open data platform, Data Footprint Network This website ensures access to reliable and publicly available information, a renowned organization dedicated to sustainability research and advocacy. To extract the information, I established a connection to an API, which provided comprehensive ecological footprint metrics for various countries. Prior to analysis, I conducted thorough data integrity checks and preprocessing steps to ensure the accuracy and consistency of the dataset. The data provided is clean, however, it is necessary to address the table formatting to long format. As well as integrating other datasets for comprehensive analysis.

Methodology

The Ecological footprint is a measure of human impact on the environment, encompassing factors such as resource consumption, waste generation, land use change, and carbon emissions. Therefore, we will find ecological footprint measures like carbon footprint, built-up land, Forest, Croland or Grazing and Fhisheries.

The Total Ecological Footprint (EF) consumption would be the sum of the ecological footprint production and the net ecological footprint of trade. Where the EF-production is the sum of bioproductive areas, a measure that measure that mirror the Gross domestic product.

Biocapacity refers to the Earth's capacity to regenerate renewable resources and absorb waste, such as carbon dioxide emissions, within a specific area and time period. It is measured in terms of hectares of biologically productive land and water.

An Ecological deficit occurs when a population's demand for ecological resources and services exceeds the biocapacity available within its territory.

Ecological reserve refers to the surplus of biologically productive land and water area within a region compared to its ecological footprint. In other words, it represents the capacity of the region's ecosystems to regenerate resources and absorb waste, exceeding the demands placed on them by human activities.

The world population has experienced significant growth, as represented by the green line. Seemingly following a linear trend. However, upon closer examination, the growth rate indicates a slight deceleration, suggesting that while the population continues to expand, it is doing so at a slower pace compared to previous years.

Simultaneously, the red line representing ecological footprint consumption shows a slight increase in demand per capita. This indicates that despite the slowdown in population growth, the individual consumption of resources is on the rise

However, it's important to note that this global perspective masks significant variations among countries. For instance, in the United States, there has been a slight decrease in demand per capita, while in China, it has increased. These differences likely reflect variations in economic performance and shifts in population demographics.

It's crucial to recognize that despite the increase in demand, the supply of natural resources remains finite. Therefore, even as consumption rises, the capacity of ecosystems to regenerate those resources may be diminishing, posing sustainability challenges for the future.

Globally, the predominant use of land resources is for carbon sequestration or energy production. This includes areas designated for fossil fuel extraction, renewable energy infrastructure, and carbon sinks. Following carbon-related land use, a significant portion of land is dedicated to food production. This includes agricultural land used for crop cultivation, livestock grazing, and aquaculture.

Forest land ranks third in terms of global land usage, encompassing areas of natural forest cover as well as managed forests for timber production, conservation, and ecosystem services.

While these global trends offer a broad overview, there are significant regional variations in land usage patterns. For instance, countries with abundant natural resources may prioritize energy production, while agrarian economies may allocate more land for food cultivation. Additionally, socio-economic factors, policy decisions, and environmental considerations influence how countries manage their land resources.

Ecological deficit occurs when a population's demand for ecological resources and services surpasses the biocapacity available within its territory. This imbalance necessitates the importation of resources to meet the deficit, placing additional strain on global ecosystems.

Prior to 1970, human reliance on Earth's resources was manageable, with extraction rates aligned with the planet's regenerative abilities. However, post-1970, there has been a stark shift. The escalation of resource utilization, including timber, freshwater, and arable land, has accelerated beyond Earth's replenishment rate. This heightened consumption has led to widespread habitat degradation, deforestation, soil erosion, and loss of biodiversity.

In summary, since 1970, we've been using Earth's resources faster than it can replace them, causing damage to the environment and making it harder for life to thrive on our planet.

The consequences of our unsustainable resource utilization are profound and far-reaching. Deforestation has displaced wildlife, disrupted ecosystems, and compromised carbon sequestration. Diminishing vegetation cover has exacerbated climate change, impacting weather patterns and air quality. Furthermore, soil degradation threatens agricultural productivity, exacerbating food insecurity and undermining ecosystem resilience.

In general, countries with high gross domestic products (GDPs) are often in ecological deficit due to higher levels of consumption and resource-intensive lifestyles. Additionally, countries that are densely populated relative to their land area may face ecological deficits, as the demand for resources exceeds the region's carrying capacity.
